WHAT IS AN INFLATABLE LIFEJACKET?

Lifejackets have saved lives at sea for decades by giving a person in the water the extra buoyancy to stay afloat. You may either wear a lifejacket where the needed displacement element is at all-time present. Or, you may have a lifejacket that creates extra buoyancy by inflating a bladder using compressed gas, a so called inflatable lifejacket. 

The inflator initiates the release of compressed gas, either automatically in water or manually, by pulling a handle. Inflatable lifejackets are the primary choice for users requiring better mobility and high flotation due to worn gear and the state of the sea.

Inflatable lifejackets are considered personal safety equipment and should be serviced and controlled regularly.

Lifejacket Standards

Lifejackets have many names depending on the country, intended area of use, and approval standard. Here are some examples: PFD (Personal Flotation Device), LPU (Life Preserving Unit), Life Preserver, Life Vest, or even Mae West.

Different lifejackets are suitable for different conditions and areas at sea, and there are different standards for levels of use. Inflatable jackets are primarily used in more demanding applications as they allow a lifejacket with high buoyancy (>150N) to be compact, slim, and more comfortable to wear. Inflatable lifejackets with high buoyancy (>150N) also often allow for the displacement to be positioned favorably for turning a person face up and floating high in the water.

Standards for lifejackets

There are several different organizations setting standards for lifejackets. Depending on the area of use, the correct standard should be chosen. Here are some of the more common standards:

ISO lifejacket standards

The ISO defines Personal Flotation Devices (PFD) in ISO 12402. The standard is commonly recognized and used by many manufacturers. It consists of four Performance Levels (PL):

  • PL 275 (ISO 12402-2:2020). Provides at least 275N of buoyancy for offshore use in severe conditions when protective clothing is being used, or when additional loads are being carried. The lifejacket should provide high flotation and be able to turn an unconscious person to face up. 
  • PL 150 (ISO 12402-3:2020). Provides at least 150N of buoyancy for offshore or rough water use, or when the users are fully clothed. This is the most common flotation level, often designed so that an unconscious person will turn face up.
  • PL 100 (ISO 12402-4:2020). Provides at least 100N of buoyancy for use in sheltered or calm water, or when the users are fully clothed.
  • PL 50 (ISO 12402-5:2020). Provides at least 50N of buoyancy for use in sheltered waters. This level is usually called buoyancy aid.

SOLAS lifejacket standards

The IMO also defines standards for inflatable lifejackets under the SOLAS regulation. SOLAS is for commercial users and applies in vessels of more than 500 gross tonnage.

The IMO resolution 48(66) set the functional requirements for a SOLAS-approved lifejacket. The European Union has adopted and approved these standards under the Marine Equipment Directive (MED). The latest directive was introduced in July 2010.

WHAT DOES AN INFLATOR DO?

The inflator is where the energy for puncturing the gas cylinder is stored or generated. An automatic inflator will commonly store the energy needed in a spring under tension held by a water-sensitive element. The element will break at entry into the water, releasing the spring connected to a pin, piercing the cylinder, and releasing gas into the bladder. The bladder increases in volume and creates buoyancy. A manual activation either releases the energy or generates the force needed to puncture the cylinder with the pull of the handle.

Measuring the pressure

The automatic inflator consists of a hydrostatic valve, a water-sensitive element holding a spring, and a gas cylinder. When the inflator is exposed to sufficient differential pressure, it will open its hydrostatic valve, allowing the water to meet the water-sensitive element and start the activation.

The differential pressure is the difference between the air pressure inside the bladder and the water pressure outside the bladder. Therefore, air inside must be limited to a minimum and free to move to the bladder's highest point.

The inflator uses the pressure difference between the internal and external pressure of the inflator. The water depth of the inflator determines the external pressure, while the trapped air bubble in the lifejacket bladder determines the internal pressure. There is always some air left, even in a well-vacuumed lifejacket bladder.

When the lifejacket enters the water, the air bubble inside the bladder will move to the highest point of the bladder. If some part of the lifejacket is above the water, the air bubble will move to that area. If the whole lifejacket is submerged, the air bubble will move to the highest point closest to the surface.

The pressure difference is determined by the depth of the inflator and the highest point where the trapped air bubbles have gathered. If this distance is more than ten centimeters, the inflator will activate, and the lifejacket inflates.

The actual activating mechanism consists of two parts:

Activating Mechanism

ACTIVATING MECHANISM

The activating mechanism, formally known as "cap", is the first of two parts in the Hammar Inflation Systems for inflatable lifejackets. The cap is the front part of the system where the energy and activating mechanism is located, for manual or automatic activation, depending on the model. When the cap is activated, it transfers force to the piercing mechanism, formally known as "body" with a cylinder that releases CO2 gas into the lifejacket bladder. The increased volume of the bladder increases the buoyancy of the lifejacket, preventing the person in the water from drowning. 

The activating mechanism uses different technologies: Automatic hydrostatic, manual, or electrical. All mechanisms can be activated manually bý the pull of a handle.

  • Automatic hydrostatic activating mechanisms use a rolled-up spring for storing the force to be delivered to the piercing mechanism. The spring is tensioned by two arms held together by a paper-element. As water enters the inflator, the paper breaks, the arms release, and the coil transfers its energy to the piercing unit. The manual activation caused by the pull in the manual release handle will break the paper and proceed the same path as an automatic activation. 
  • Manual activating mechanisms create a rotational force as the manual release handle is pulled. The force is transferred through the shaft to the piercing mechanism.

Electrical activating mechanisms use several sensors to determine when to activate. Read more about our COR Electronic Inflation System here.

Piercing Mechanism

CYLINDER AND PIERCING MECHANISM

The piercing mechanism, formally known as "body" with a cylinder, is the second of two parts in the Hammar Inflation Systems for inflatable lifejackets. The body contains the means for piercing the cylinder to release CO2 gas into the lifejacket bladder. The increased volume of the bladder increases the buoyancy of the lifejacket, preventing the person in the water from drowning.

The Automatic and Manual Inflator Systems use a sharp pin that is mounted on an eccentric axle. When the rotating force from the activating mechanism is applied to the eccentric axle, the pin will penetrate the gas cylinder and return to its original position. Leaving a distinct hole and plenty of space provides a fast activation and allows for the cool gas to pass. This minimizes the risk of ice crystals forming and blocking the gas' path and is important, especially in cold climates.

Tests show that Hammar's piercing mechanism is superior in cold weather activation.

The COR Electronic Inflator System uses a brand new patented method for cylinder piercing. Read more about the COR piercing technology here

Protected gas cylinder

The gas cylinder is placed inside the inflatable bladder. This protects it from the atmosphere, corrosive effects of saltwater, and reduces snagging hazards. Extreme cold conditions will not affect the hydrostatic system and moisture cannot enter to form ice crystals. Furthermore, the CO2 does not have to enter through a valve to inflate the lifejacket, which also minimizes the risk of ice crystals forming.

On the Hammar MA1 and Hammar M2 piercing mechanism, the cylinder is permanently fixed to prevent unwinding.

MA1 / M2 Rearming Instructions Expand

BEFORE YOU START REARMING

Make sure you have a smooth, flat, and dry work surface.

  1. Insert the opening key between the black locking ring and the yellow cap. Turn the key anticlockwise. The black locking ring will now turn anticlockwise.
  2. Hold the yellow cap with one hand and continue to turn the black locking ring anticlockwise with the other hand until it stops.
  3. Lift the yellow cap. Remove the old inflator body and gas cylinder from inside the bladder and dispose of it in an environmentally friendly manner.
  4. Check the Rearming Kit. Ensure that all the following four points are met before you continue.
  5. • The single point indicator on the inflator cap is green. ( )
  6. • The indicator on the black body is green. ( )
  7. • The expiry date on the inflator cap is within the limit.
  8. • The size of the gas cylinder is correct.
  9. Do not assemble any cap or body that does not meet these requirements.
  10. Insert the inflator body with the gas cylinder pointing upwards in the bladder. Let the sealing ring rest on the rounded sealing surface on the body.
  11. Position the replacement cap with the hydrostatic valve pointing to the right, 90° relative to the gas cylinder, and put the cap in place on the body.
  12. Press firmly on the inflator cap and turn the black locking ring clockwise into the locked position.

FINAL CHECK

  • Pull the cap to make sure it has locked on the inflator body properly.
  • Check that the locking ring is properly locked and cannot be turned anticlockwise by hand.
  • Inflate the lifejacket through the oral tube. Check that it stays inflated at least overnight.

PACKING

Before packing, deflate the lifejacket through the oral tube. Make sure all residual air is pressed out from the bladder to assure the best activating conditions for the inflator and that it can be folded properly according to the manufacturer's instruction. Make sure that the lifejacket is dry before packing.

6. Can I clean my lifejacket? Expand

You must wash your lifejacket according to the instructions from the lifejacket manufacturer.

  • The Hammar Inflator will withstand a normal rinse with the lifejacket hanging.
  • For more thorough washing, always remove the inflator. After washing, let the lifejacket dry before rearming (see rearming instructions).
  • Do not dry clean.
  • When not in use, always store your lifejacket in a dry and ventilated space.
7. Can I replace my automatic inflator with a manual one? Expand

All Hammar Lifejacket Inflators share the same interface with the bladder. So yes, it is possible. Check with the lifejacket manufacturer to verify that the certification allows for conversion.

8. Can I bring my Hammar inflator with me when flying? Expand

The Hammar inflator is not affected by the pressure differences created in the air.

Whether or not you are allowed to bring it onto an aeroplane is up to the airline. Inflatable lifejackets are considered life-saving appliances, inflatable, and should generally not be an issue to bring onboard. There is an inflatable lifejacket stored under each seat of the aeroplane as demonstrated in the pre-flight safety demonstration.

Check with your airline before flying and ask for their specific recommendations on how you may transport your inflatable life-saving appliance.
